SQL · Data Analysis

Bank Customer Churn Analysis

Business Problem

A retail bank is losing customers without a clear understanding of which segments are most at risk or why they are leaving. This project uses structured query analysis across 10,000 customer records to identify the key drivers of churn — segmented by geography, age, product holdings, and complaint history — and produces actionable retention recommendations.

Key Finding: Customers who filed a complaint had a 99.5% churn rate versus 0.1% for those who had not — making complaint history a near-certain predictor of customer exit.

Customer Churn Executive Dashboard

The SQL analysis revealed what was happening — this dashboard makes it impossible to ignore, putting the findings in the hands of a bank manager who can act on them in real time.

I built a two-page interactive Power BI dashboard on the same 10,000-customer dataset, with a DAX Churn Rate measure that recalculates dynamically with every slicer interaction. Page 1 covers the executive summary; Page 2 goes deeper into behavioural factors and a stacked risk segment analysis that identifies the highest-priority retention target in the data.

Outcome: Stacking three risk factors — German geography, age 45–59, and a single product holding — identifies a customer segment with a 74.8% churn rate: nearly 3 in 4 will leave.

Kenya County Economic Growth & Poverty Analysis

Business Problem

County governments and policymakers often use GDP-style growth figures as a proxy for development, but percentage growth can mislead when it starts from a low economic base. This project analyzes official Gross County Product (GCP) data alongside World Bank poverty estimates across all 47 Kenyan counties to test whether fast-growing counties are actually reducing poverty — or just growing off a smaller base.

Key Finding: Counties with the fastest GCP growth (2013–2020) — Marsabit, Mandera, Elgeyo-Marakwet, West Pokot — actually show higher poverty rates, not lower. Absolute economic scale, not growth rate, is the stronger predictor of low poverty: Nairobi and Mombasa combine large established economies with the lowest poverty in the country.
